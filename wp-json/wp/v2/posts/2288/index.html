\nMarch 16, 1927
\nLos Angeles\n<\/p>\n

\n \n<\/p>\n

\n \n<\/p>\n

\n \n<\/p>\n

\nClarence and Ona Brown were married in 1922, but now Ona wants a divorce. \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cWhen I married him,\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d said Mrs. Brown, while weeping bitterly during her testimony before Judge Summerfield, \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201che was a second-rate assistant director, and I made a director<\/a> out of him. That cost me my home, for he got to thinking so well of himself he attempted to boss the house. He went nearly a year without even speaking to me.\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d\n<\/p>\n

\n \n<\/p>\n

\n \n<\/p>\n

\n(She may have a point; see this page<\/a> under "salary.")\n<\/p>\n

\n \n<\/p>\n

\nOna’s testimony was neither denied nor contested, and she won her decree. \n<\/p>\n

\n \n<\/p>\n

\n \n<\/p>\n

\nThink of that<\/em> the next time you watch "Garbo’s favorite director<\/a>".\n<\/p>\n
