\nMarch 9, 1927
\nBorneo
\n\n<\/p>\n

\nOn the one hand, the Dutch government\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s Eurocentric cultural imperialism is wiping out the native culture of Borneo; on the other, they\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2re teaching headhunters the benefits of recycling. <\/p>\n

The Dyak<\/a> have long needed a nice fresh human caput for any and every sort of local ritual, but the generations-long war against the practice by their clogphilic, decapitated-headphobic overlords, has shown them the benefits of conservation (long practiced in the densely populated liberal parliamentary democracy that is Holland).<\/p>\n

It is reported that now, the Dyak just borrow heads from neighbors for celebrations such as the birth of a child or the ending of a period of mourning. In fact, a paltry few heads are passed around in a tribe where just a generation ago each family had a bag of its own.<\/p>\n

(According to Dr. William O. Krohn<\/a>, Chicago alienist, there\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s still no lack of headhunting when absolutely necessary\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u20ac\u009da rajah\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s death demands craniums, for example, and victims thus beheaded are regarded as members of the monarch\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s heavenly retinue.)<\/p>\n

Despite the Dyak’s newfound respect for Westernized ideals, Borneo\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s 1957 independence from windmills and tulips removed<\/a> such colonialist standards of civility. The wild and wooly Americas, though, never had a break from<\/a> her<\/a> fascination<\/a> with<\/a> beheading<\/a>.\n<\/p>\n
