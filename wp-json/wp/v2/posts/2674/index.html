\n
<\/a>July 12, 1947
Los Angeles<\/p>\n

Was it shame, pride or loss of blood that made Dorothy Hawkins, 24-year-old waitress, half-rise from her General Hospital bed to grit her teeth and insist the man (tall, slim, 20s, brown suit) who shot her as she sat on a bus bench at Wilshire and Vermont<\/a> was a stranger? And the stranger was polite. After shooting her, he said \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cI am sorry that happened. I\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2ll go and call and ambulance.\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d<\/p>\n

But when police visited Miss Hawkins\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2 apartment at 1214 W. 8th Street<\/a>, they found the door smashed in. Neighbor Frances Darby described a man busting it down, arguing with Hawkins, and leaving with some boxes not long before the 2:30am shooting.<\/p>\n

Former Folsom prisoner Aubrey Jones, 26, was raised on the horn and asked to come down to the station. He admitted he\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2d spent some time with Miss Hawkins, but declined to meet officers as requested. A bulletin was issued for his arrest.<\/p>\n

Meanwhile, the lady has less than a 50% chance of surviving. Gut shots are tricky that way.<\/p>\n
