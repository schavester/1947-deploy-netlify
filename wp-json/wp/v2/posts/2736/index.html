We tend to think of bowling alleys as full of googiefied grandeur, replete with folded-plate roofs and flagcrete walls, tapering pylons and swiss-cheesed I-beams, the assorted amoeboid whatnot and odd illuminated starburst, surmounted, or course, with a giant neon bowling pin. (America\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s arguably most famous of such bowling alleys, the giant neon bowling pin-surmounted Hollywood Star Lanes, star of The Big Lebowski<\/em>, was recently demo\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2d by the LAUSD to build Planet-Killer Elementary #6.)<\/p>\n

But there was a time when bowling alleys were of Italianate design, or Moderne, or, in the case of the Sunset Center, grand Neoclassical affairs with giant fluted columns.<\/p>\n



The eagle-eye\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2d may ascertain where the coffee shop neon was once affixed to what is now the HQ of Tribune Entertainment:
<\/p>\n

There are fewer bowling alleys in LA than bald eagles, and\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2re twice as endangered. An endangered, passing world. A world you\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2ll never know. A world where the damn rep from Red Crown Ten Pins is late; he\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2ll wanna try and talk you into replacing your Ebonites with his Mineralite balls again, and the American guy hasn\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2t fixed the Backus pin spotter so you\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2re gonna call Brunswick for a quote on a 20th spotter and a Telescore projector while you\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2re at it, and hey, maybe that cat from Acme Billiard who plays league games Tuesday can give you the skinny on refinishing your maple.
