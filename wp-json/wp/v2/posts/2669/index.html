July 16, 1947
Lincoln Heights<\/p>\n

Visiting the in-laws can be such a drag. But there\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s no excuse for behaving like the late Raymond Scott did during his stay with Mrs. Eva Utzinger, 74-year-old mother of Scott\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s widow Lucille.<\/p>\n

The Scotts were on vacation at the Utzinger home, 368 S. Ave. 21<\/a>, when Eva turned up with a broken skull at the foot of the outside stairs leading into the cellar. Detectives found a red-stained hatchet in the cellar, and turned it over to the lab to see if the stains were blood.<\/p>\n

Also nearby, an unsigned note in Scott\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s handwriting. \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cI must end the source of trouble in this house. You know whom I mean. I find I cannot escape a breakdown. And so, I am jumping from the N. Broadway Bridge<\/a>. You have been a wonderful family. Here is insurance to get you well.\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d<\/p>\n

Scott, who was 60, and a schoolteacher at Taft, was indeed found gravely injured at the base of the Broadway and Pasadena Avenue bridge. He died at Hollywood Presbyterian<\/a>. Lucille confirmed that both she and her son had been ill.<\/p>\n

<\/a><\/p>\n
