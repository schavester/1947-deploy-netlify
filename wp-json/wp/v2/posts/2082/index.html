August 21, 1927
Los Angeles <\/p>\n

The voice on the phone was breathless. "Help! Murder! Police!! He’s killing me, come quick!" (All dialogue, however fanciful, comes verbatim from today’s Los Angeles Times<\/em>.) "Be right over," responded the dispatcher, as he sent two of his "star murder squad officers" to the scene of the crime. <\/p>\n

They were met at the door of 1618 East 27th Street<\/a> by Mrs. Georgia Brown, who quickly assured them there was nothing out of order at her house. "Must be something wrong somewhere [else], everything’s running according to Hoyle<\/a> here," she told the officers. <\/p>\n

Then Mrs. Brown made an error, a grievous one, the kind for which you’ll forever after kick yourself, even if you live to be 100. "Just walk in and see for yourselves," she said. <\/p>\n

The cops didn’t need to be asked twice. A five-minute investigation of Mrs. Brown’s house turned up five bottles of home brew. Called before the judge on a charge of possessing alcohol, Mrs. Brown was given the choice of a $100 fine or ten days in jail. She chose the former—though she still believes the police came to the wrong house. <\/p>\n<\/div>\n<\/div>\n","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"
