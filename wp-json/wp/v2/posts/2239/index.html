January 20, 1927
Los Angeles<\/p>\n

A short notice in the paper today about Sidney (or Sydney) Adams who, on August 2, 1925 (most likely) mortally shot his wife Annie in their home at 1234 East Twenty-First Street<\/a>.<\/p>\n

Despite there being a chance for a difference—Adams steadily asserted that the woman committed suicide<\/a>—on October 12, 1925 it took a Los Angeles jury a record twenty-five minutes to send him to the gallows.  (This being in part or wholly dependent on Adams’ race seems obvious—writ large as he’s routinely described as the “giant negro,” a term of which Times seems unusually fond<\/a>.)<\/p>\n

Hangings were on the mind of all Californians as executions ushered in C. C. Young<\/a>’s gubernatorial regime. The previous four years of Friend Richardson<\/a>’s governorship were marked by constant rejections of eleventh-hour appeals for executive clemency; in a show of consistency Young<\/a> had five executions in the first five weeks of his stewardship and saw that each one went through unchallenged.  <\/p>\n

<\/p>\n

There were six sitting in San Quentin’s death row when S. C. Stone<\/a>  joined the bunch on January 6, 1927—making it lucky number seven.  Adams’ departure today took it back down to six.<\/p>\n<\/div>\n<\/div>\n","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"
