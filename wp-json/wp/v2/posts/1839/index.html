\n
<\/a>
\nJan. 13, 1907
\n<\/span>Los Angeles
\n<\/span>
\n<\/span>The Times takes a light, humorous look at the destructive wanderings of Eaton Wash: a docile stream, if not entirely dry, most of the year, turned into a churning monster by heavy rains.
\n<\/span>
\n<\/span>\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cThe little river that makes so much trouble lives somewhere in the fastness of Eaton\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s Canyon during the summer months,\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d The Times says. \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cIn the rainy season it always comes prowling out for a wild outing.
\n<\/span>
\n<\/span><\/a>
\n<\/span>\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cNot having a respectable bed like other rivers, it comes bursting down from the mountains and goes wherever anyone will let it go.
\n<\/span>
\n<\/span>\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cWhen the rains began this year, it stole trustfully\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u20ac\u009dundiscouraged by its previous disappointments\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u20ac\u009ddown from the mountains.
\n<\/span>
\n<\/span>\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cIt sneaked on a pleasant-looking ranch in the valley. And the farmer found it there\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u20ac\u009das it covered about half his ranch\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u20ac\u009dand rushed out with shovels and teams and turned it back on someone else\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s ranch.
\n<\/span>
\n<\/span>\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cAnd the ranchman who owns this second ranch on which it was driven came out in a rage and shook his fist, bellowing: \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00cb\u0153Here, come and take back your old river. It can\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2t stay on my place.\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2 And he said other things.\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d
\n<\/span>
\n<\/span>The engineers of the Southern Pacific railroad built a massive culvert to protect the tracks from washing out. But another neighbor, Annie Adams, hired men to turn the fences on her 36-acre ranch into a barrier.
\n<\/span>
\n<\/span>\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cThe only way for the river to get on Miss Adams\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2 ranch was to jump the fence,\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d The Times says.
\n<\/span>
\n<\/span>\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cAnyone familiar with the eccentricities [of the river] can guess readily enough what happened then,\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d The Times says. \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cThat fine and elegant new culvert of the railroad company lasted about a minute.\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d
\n<\/span>
\n<\/span>More men and teams of horses built a barrier of sandbags to stop the river. \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cIt went. It rippled forlornly down the side of the high embanked track trying to find a hole through the sandbag dike\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u20ac\u009dbut nothing doing.
\n<\/span>
\n<\/span>\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cThen it came to a long, hard, fascinating looking strip of road leading through the middle of the little town of Savannah. It turned down this road with a little gurgle of joy,\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d The Times says.
\n<\/span>
\n<\/span>\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cAnd the things that it did to that county road is enough to make the county supervisors weep in anguish.
\n<\/span>
\n<\/span>\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cIt was a beautiful oiled road\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u20ac\u009dsmooth and flat and even. The river gouged out chuckholes as deep as a well. It made ruts in which you could drydock a ship. It ruined several hundred dollars\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2 worth of highway in less time than it takes to write it.\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d
\n<\/span>
\n<\/span>Lawsuits followed and a committee was appointed to pick a course for the river. But none of the ranchers wanted the river on his property.
\n<\/span>
\n<\/span>\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cIn the end, the court will undoubtedly select a straight, direct route, with the proper angle and fall and slope from the canyon to the river, and order a right of way condemned\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u20ac\u009dlet it cross whose ranch it may,\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d The Times says.<\/p>\n

Bonus factoid: The story uses the phrase “No. 23 skiddoo,” which I’ve always associated with the 1920s. Apparently the anonymous writer was ahead of his time.
