Mission-style auto courts. Googie bowling alleys. Roadside burger stands. Neon liquor store signs. Stiles Clements buildings. All are remodeled or demo\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2d with soul-crushing regularity. Of the hundreds that were and the handful that are, maybe, just maybe, should Fortuna bless us with her grace, we will be granted a precious one of each for our progeny to savor.<\/p>\n

They are the few out of many. But there is only one Bradbury Building. <\/p>\n

Yes, it\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s a pleasant sandstone & dressed brickwork Romanesque commercial structure. Gorgeous opal-glass blade sign. And when you go inside\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u20ac\u009dor rent Blade Runner<\/em> if you\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2re lazy\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u20ac\u009dthere you\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2ll witness the jaw-dropping skylit interior atrium, the acres of wrought iron filigree, the glazed hydraulic elevators. And you think, where the hell did this come from?<\/p>\n

LA, 1890. Bradbury hires famed LA architect Sumner Hunt to design an office building. Hunt gets to work, and presents the finished drawings to Bradbury. Bradbury doesn\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2t care for them, and so tosses the project in the lap of a young draftsman in Hunt\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s firm, one George Wyman. Wyman isn\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2t an architect, but he has<\/em> read Bellamy\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s recently published utopian novel Looking Backward<\/em>, which describes buildings of the year 2000 (they’re \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cbathed in light”). After consulting the Ouija board to make sure he should take the project (his dead brother said to go ahead), the li\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2l autodidact jumped in with both feet.<\/p>\n

Which was of course scheduled for razing in the 70s (as were the Watts Towers in the 60s, another piece of LA both-footism) \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u20ac\u0153 and hell, this was a building described in 19-Victorian-demolishing-47<\/em> as \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201chistoric.\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d But then mighty State Senator Jim \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cMills Act\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d Mills championed the California Historic Building Code, which rescued the Bradbury and countless others from the raw maw of demolition.<\/p>\n

The building deteriorated until beautifully restored by the late Ira Yellin in the early 90s. Hong Kong real estate mogul Goodwin Yaw has since picked up the building and is planning a nightclub right there on the ground floor. The building that Minnie Epp helped save and Joe Stovall gave a foot for, this building that represents man\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s noblest achievements, this building that is actually as close to being alive as any of us \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u20ac\u0153 will be rattled by booming bass, doused with sticky drinks, punched full of outlets, tagged and bruised and burnt: does anybody else consider this a really bad idea?<\/p><\/div>\n<\/div>\n","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"
