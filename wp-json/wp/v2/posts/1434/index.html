<\/a>
\n<\/o:p>Jan. 28, 1907
\nLos Angeles <\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

William Jennings Bryan stepped from the Owl train to be greeted by a long-waiting crowd. <\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cIn appearance, Mr. Bryan has changed but little since he was last in Los Angeles,\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d The Times says. \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cIn his manner, also, there has been little, if any, change, and he greeted his friends with the same fervor and showed the same remarkable talent for remembering names.\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d <\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

From the Arcade Station, Bryan and his wife were transported by auto to the home of Nathan Cole on Pasadena Avenue. They took the Mt. Lowe railway and in the evening, he addressed a capacity audience at Simpson Auditorium in a benefit for the Lark Ellen Home for Boys.<\/a> <\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

At 47, Bryan was no longer the fiery orator of his youth, The Times says. Instead, he was a gentle idealist who \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201ctalked of the thousand little things that had found his favor on five continents, and a packed audience listened with almost breathless attention.\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d <\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cThey liked the esthetic idealism of this older Bryan,\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d The Times says of the audience. \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cAll along the many curving rows of seats, there was a leaning forward, as if to catch some word that had been lost, and a whispering sigh of regret.\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d <\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cIn soft, sweet periods, reminiscent of \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00cb\u0153Gray\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s Elegy,\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2 he lauded the age of belief, the age of dreams. Touchingly, he quoted from John Boyle O\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2Reilly, \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00cb\u0153For the dreamer lives forever, but the toiler dies in a day.\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2 \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d <\/span><\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cWilliam Jennings Bryan, making a simple discourse of so pretentious a subject as \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00cb\u0153The Old World and Its Ways\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2 showed himself still, as in the promulgation of strong beliefs that lie near his heart, the apostle of the past.\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d <\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

The next day, there was a trip to Santa Catalina Island for the Bryans and 100 guests, followed by banquet hosted by local Democrats. Before leaving for Salt Lake City, he addressed the students of Polytechnic High School and attended a reception at the Chamber of Commerce. <\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

Eighteen years later, Bryan and Clarence Darrow met in Dayton, Tenn., for the Scopes Monkey Trial.<\/a> He died two days after the trial\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s conclusion. <\/p>\n
