February 24, 1927
\nLos Angeles<\/p>\n

In what the papers have dubbed the \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cMustard-Seed Murder,\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d we see once again that, gentlemen, your sartorial decisions are always of importance. (Damn trouser cuffs! Were this 1960, this condemned man would\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2ve walked free.)<\/p>\n

The murder in question dates back to October 20, 1926, when one Charles O. Westcott, 63 years of age and scion of General Grant, opened his door at 909 S. Cochran St. only to met by a gun-wielding assailant. Blam! Blam!\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u20ac\u009done to the heart, one in the stomach. Unfortunately for Charles\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2 son Carl, Charles\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2 dying words to his new wife were \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cCarl shot me.\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d<\/p>\n

<\/a><\/p>\n

Apparently Carl, 40, was vexed that his father left a $300,000 ($3,486,687 USD2007) trust to the aforementioned new wife Hazel. And then there was the matter of the $100,000 trust that Carl\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s grandfather was to leave to his son Charles\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u00a6but with Carl\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s father Charles out of the way\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u00a6it would go straight to Carl.<\/p>\n

<\/a><\/p>\n

Detectives didn\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2t really buy Carl\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s long and rambling alibi, involving a gambler\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s den at Seventh and San Pedro, and a dance hall on Hill off Sixth. Nor did they take his alibi\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s verification for much, given as it came from a bootlegger and some other underworld habitu\u00c3\u0192\u00c2\u00a9s. Despite Carl\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s regal upbringing, he\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s a part-time barber with a record, having done time in Folsom and Stillwater on forgery charges. <\/p>\n

And so today begins Westcott the Younger\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s trial. The crux of the trial comes down to the mustard seeds found in his trouser cuffs\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u20ac\u009dthe assassin was spotted escaping through the the vacant lot to the north (now 905 South Cochran, not built upon til 1928), and dang\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2d if Carl wasn\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2t found with a few inconspicuous mustard seeds and a half-dozen broken blades of grass, indentical to those from the lot, in his cuffs. (Westcott also contended that he tore his trousers at the knee while tripping on the rough steps of that Sixth Street dive; investigation revealed those steps to be carpeted; the prosecution points to a plank with in the lot with matching fibers.)
\n<\/a><\/p>\n

He is convicted March 2, and March 9 sentenced to life imprisonment at Folsom; two more trials on points of law (and a botched suicide attempt) failed to free him. But his March 1929 sanity trial got him sprung from Folsom and committed to the State Hospital at Norwalk, to be held until such time as he was mentally competent to go on trial again for the murder charge. <\/p>\n

As there\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s no further mention of Westcott again, we can only assume he elected to stay put. <\/p>\n<\/div>\n<\/div>\n","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"
