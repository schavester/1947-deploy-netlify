December 5, 1927<\/p>\n

Mr. and Mrs. Jack Laughlin of 2115 S. Harvard departed for a weekend getaway in San Diego, leaving their daughter, June Blossom, 14, in the care of their housekeeper and family friends.  After saying goodbye to her folks, June invited her friend Mary Jane Carroll, 13, over for the weekend.<\/p>\n

Sunday afternoon, the girls went outside to play, and vanished.  Shortly after their disappearance was noticed, the blue dress and sandals that June had been wearing that day were found in a nearby vacant lot.  When the Laughlins returned, they found that in addition to a missing daughter, about $4000 worth of clothing and tapestries were missing from their home.<\/p>\n

So sinister-sounding were the facts surrounding the disappearance of Mary Jane, and June that it seems impossible that the incident wrapped up as happily as it did.  As it turns out that the whole thing could be chalked up to a case of "girls will be girls."<\/p>\n

On December 6, Mr. Laughlin and Mr. Carroll set out to pick up their daughters from a San Diego hotel.  The girls had skipped town on a lark with the intention of surprising June’s parents in San Diego.  Unfortunately, they’d left around the same time that Mr. and Mrs. Laughlin had started home.<\/p>\n

No word on how June’s clothes turned up in the lot, or the whereabouts of the missing tapestries; however, the most precious cargo was accounted for, albeit in deep, deep trouble.<\/p>\n<\/div>\n<\/div>\n","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"
