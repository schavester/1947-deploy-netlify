\nMarch 5, 1907
\nLos Angeles <\/p>\n

What shall we do with Emma? She\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s gone off to New Mexico and married a Chinaman. Her horrified mother hopes to get the marriage annulled, but Emma is an independent-minded young lady.<\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

Emma\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s mother, Mary Culver of Monrovia, says she will do everything she can to undo her daughter\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s marriage to Frank Chew, whom The Times describes as \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201ca sort of missionary revivalist,\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d noting that \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cMiss Emma had longings to help the heathen herself.\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d<\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

Chew asked Culver for permission to marry Emma, but \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cit was bluntly refused,\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d The Times says. \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cEmma had a mind of her own and her answer was \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00cb\u0153yes,\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2 regardless of her mother\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s wishes.\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d <\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

Even worse, Chew could be an illegal immigrant and if he\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s deported, Emma says she will be willing to go to China with him. She made this vow, even though she was warned that Chew would sell her into white slavery as soon as he got her to China.<\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

Emma isn\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2t the only one in trouble. Members of the Chinese Baptist Mission are equally furious, saying that Chew borrowed jewelry from members of the congregation under the pretense of defending himself against deportation when in fact he used the money for his elopement. <\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

In August 1907, Emma sent a letter from Hong Kong to her family, saying that their fears were baseless and that she and her husband had opened a day school where they taught English. <\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

The next year, Emma mailed a photograph of her students and tried to recruit more women to come to China. <\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cWith her husband, Frank Chew, she has established an English school which is attended by the sons of well-to-do, educated Chinese gentlemen. The Chews have prospered beyond their wildest dreams,\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d The Times says. <\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cEvery family in Hong Kong seems anxious to have its children learn English and the pupils themselves study the language eagerly.\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d<\/p>\n
