March 1, 1907
\nLos Angeles<\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

Downtown businessmen are at a complete loss over what to do with the garbage from their operations and want the city to either take it or designate a dump they can use. <\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cThey declare that the Board of Health has refused to let further deposits of garbage or refuse be made at the old dumping ground to the southeast of the city and state that if the city does not come forward with a proposition to locate a new dump, or to cremate the stuff, they will be helpless to get rid of the accumulations of each day\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s business,\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d The Times says. <\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

City officials say they don\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2t know what to do because the local sanitation system is strictly for residential use. The Times says that local ordinances define garbage as \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201canimal and vegetable refuse from the kitchens.\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d As a result, officials feel no need to deal with commercial waste, although they concede it is a problem. <\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cWe can scarcely take care of the garbage we are now forced to collect, one Board of Public Works commissioner says. \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cWe don\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2t want to take care of any more of it, even if it is hauled to the crematory without cost to the city.\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d <\/p>\n
