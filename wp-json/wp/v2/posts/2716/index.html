June 15, 1947
Pasadena<\/p>\n

James E. Fronimos is a man of distinction\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u00a6 but who really wants to be known as the first man Pasadena cops ever booked for investigation of grand theft airplane and drunk flying?<\/p>\n

Fronimos, 24, a student pilot with a scant two hours solo air time, decided to take a joyride in a Cub Cruiser he found parked at Montebello airfield around 1:30am. He picked the Cub because it was the only plane on the field with a self-starter. His intent was to circle the field a few times and bring her on home\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u00a6 but the inexperienced pilot\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s circle developed an uncontrollable circumference, and soon he was running low on gas.<\/p>\n

While in the air, Fronimos had moved from East L.A. to Pasadena, so when he brought her down, it was on the Arroyo Parkway, near California Street<\/a>. Fortunately, he killed the ignition on the way down, thus only crumpling a wing and scratching up his face when he crashed into a light pole with a \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201chospital zone\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d sign on it, and not exploding.<\/p>\n

Officers took Fronimos to the drunk tank and towed the plane to the wrecked auto yard at Ward & Son\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s Garage.<\/p><\/div>\n<\/div>\n","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"
