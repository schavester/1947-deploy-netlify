July 8, 1947
Lincoln Heights<\/p>\n

It was 1:00 am when the divorce papers came, informing Antonio Mondragon, sheet metal worker of 1925 \u00c3\u201a\u00c2\u00bd Gates Street<\/a> that his four-year marriage to Rosenda, 20, was ending. Of course, it was no surprise\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u20ac\u009dshe\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2d moved out of the house she shared with Antonio and her sister, Mrs. Trinidad Vigil, two months previously, and was living at 826 S. Crocker Street.<\/a><\/p>\n

So the papers were served, and then about an hour later Rosenda herself appeared\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u20ac\u009ddrunk, said Antonio. They argued, and she left. Antonio followed, and saw his wife get into a car.<\/p>\n

Or did she? William Moore, market clerk, says a woman matching Rosenda\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s description called a cab from his store (location: N. Main and Mission Road<\/a>) around 2:15am, telling the dispatcher she wanted a ride to San Pedro and 9th Street<\/a> (a block from her home). But a car came by before the cab did, and the lady thumbed a ride with a husky blondish fellow in a dark coupe.
<\/span>
Flash forward a couple of hours to the early dawn, when mail clerk Newton Josha finds a gruesome package in a gutter on Elmyra Street near North Main<\/a>: Rosenda Mondragon\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s naked corpse, a silk stocking tightly tied around her throat. No signs of sexual attack. The likelihood that she had been killed elsewhere and pushed from a moving car.<\/p>\n

Officers promptly gave Antonio a lie detector test, found his story at odds with his nosy neighbors, and booked him on suspicion of murder at University Division Jail.<\/p>\n

Was it a husband scorned? The Black Dahlia\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s killer, making a more northerly assault? Or an entirely new threat to the women of Los Angeles, striking near the city\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s heart with a still-warm victim dumped by the train yards just up the road from the halls of commerce and of law? And how drunk did one have to be to thumb a ride, with Liz Short’s killer still on the loose? Drunk enough so it didn’t hurt to die?<\/p>\n

We can only hope so.<\/p><\/div>\n<\/div>\n","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"
