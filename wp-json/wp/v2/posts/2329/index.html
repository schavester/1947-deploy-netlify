I don’t know about you, no, really, I don’t know about you, but I<\/span> get hot for chemical journals what discuss that unpredictably violent and violently unpredictable perchloric acid. You’d think Bob O’Connor, as manager\/secretary of O’Connor Electro-Plating Corp., would be similarly obsesssed. You’d think wrong. Bob was a sales and business tool. When some other cat named Bob–they stick together, you know, those Bobs–when Bob Magee told him about the magic to be had from exciting your perchloric stew with electrolytes writ large, Bob O’Connor bit and bit hard. To the detriment of a few city blocks.<\/p>\n

Let\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s say<\/span> it was an isolated incident. And let\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s say we were wrong again<\/span>. <\/p>\n

Toss a stone in this town. Hit, say, Pasadena. Let\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s see what that stone wrought.<\/p>\n

A simple plating factory (the Crown City plating factory, at 28 West Union):
very nearly took out all<\/span> of what\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s now \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cOld Town\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d on 22 February, 1925. The Los Angeles Times<\/span> reported that<\/span> but then the next<\/span> near mishap, well, that got bumped up a bit:<\/p>\n

In quiet little Pasadena, Calif, one day last week a blast almost materialized that would have shaken the sober townfolk out of their skins. Two blocks from Pasadena’s busiest corner, Crown City Plating Co. electroplates chromium, gold, brass, silver, copper. A swart little man named Wallace Foreman was mixing sulphuric acid and glycerin to make an electrolyte for plating. Already in the tank were 75 gal. of acid and 2 gal. of glycerin. Thinking to add more acid, Wallace Foreman picked up a 3-gal. container, dumped in the contents. Unluckily the container held not sulphuric but nitric acid….
<\/span>
Time Magazine<\/span>, 27 August 1934<\/p>\n

I mean, you can mix sulphuric acid with glycerin all you want. Nitric acid, well, that makes nitro-glycerin. The rest writes itself. A smoldering hole three blocks wide.<\/p>\n

<\/p>\n

It had been reported that there was a particularly beautiful, and occupied, house just behind on 14th — the house was blown apart like so many tragically electroplated child’s limbs —-
<\/p>\n

This was mentioned as a particularly sticky rescue area, given this area had at the time held a giant and absurdly intricate Queen Anne mansion. The developerclass blesses every day which includes Victorian spindlework thrown sixty some-odd blocks as if touched by the Finger of God.<\/p>\n

Nigh-on sixty years in, we still<\/a> talk about this kind of “keep acid under refrigeration<\/a>” dictate.<\/p>\n
