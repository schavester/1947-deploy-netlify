I believe it was Phil Langdon who talked about the \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cbrowning of America.\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d No, that has nothing to do with shifting ethnic demographics. The mid-60s saw a reaction against Exaggerated Modern architecture, and so architects attached mansard roofs and brick facades to everything. Earth tones became common currency, and Broadway Plaza\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u20ac\u009dCharles F. Luckman Assc, 1972, designed in a sort of \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cEnvironmental Brutalist\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d style\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u20ac\u009dis a about as brown as it gets. By the 80s we\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2d returned to glass and steel, but a few hallmarks of 70s architecture, like this noble beast, remain.<\/p>\n

<\/p>\n

But for how long? While Portman\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s 1976 Bonaventure is likely to be landmarked, many High-70s buildings are being lost or compromised. Of course, one may argue that the Bonaventure is a better building than this Broadway; one may be right. But the preservationist camp owes a strange debt to Luckman\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u20ac\u009dit\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s his MSGCenter that stands atop the rubble of Penn Station, and it was in razing that building that America\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s interest in preserving her monuments was sparked. <\/p>\n

(Luckman went on to recommend tearing down Goodhue\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s library, btw.)<\/p>\n
