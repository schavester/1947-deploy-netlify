December 11, 1927
\nHollywood<\/p>\n

The death of motion-picture actor George Donald Bailey was announced this morning. The 63-year-old thespian complained of feeling ill yesterday. A doctor was summoned, but Bailey died within a few hours. The death certificate, signed by Dr. C.D. Baker (a friend of the deceased), stated the cause as heart disease. <\/p>\n

The matter would seem to rest there, but this afternoon Bailey’s widow was visited by her daughter, Blanche Olivarias, and Blanche’s sister-in-law, Miss Tommy Olivarias. The women brewed a soothing pot of coffee, sipped from their cups, and immediately were gripped by nausea. Tommy, in particular, became violently sick and felt a choking sensation in her throat. <\/p>\n

Unusual, you think, but this is where the plot thickens: “‘My husband clutched at his throat just prior to his death,’ Mrs. Bailey said. ‘He kept mumbling he was being choked to death'”\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u20ac\u009djust after having a cup of coffee from the same fatal pot. Indeed, doctors say the only reason Tommy recovered was emergency treatment. <\/p>\n

The county coroner requested a chemical analysis of Bailey’s organs. The coffee pot and whatever liquid remained in it were turned over to the county chemist. Results were expected sometime the following week. <\/p>\n

Did heart disease kill George D. Bailey\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u20ac\u009dor was it poison? Were his widow, daughter, and her sister-in-law victims of the same toxin? Alas, the Times<\/i> never reported on the outcome of the autopsy or tests on the coffee pot.\n<\/div>\n<\/div>\n","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"
