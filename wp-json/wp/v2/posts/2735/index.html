June 3, 1947
Los Angeles<\/p>\n

Max Stein may be the American Bowling Congress all-events record holder, but that hasn’t stopped Charley Bragg, president of the Los Angeles Bowling Association, from suspending Stein’s membership.<\/p>\n

The trouble started during the recent $10,000 tournament at Hollywood’s Sunset Center,<\/a> when Stein was found to have listed two fake (and doubtless high scoring!) names among the leaders. These names were discovered before the close of competition, and all winners were paid off.<\/p>\n

Stein was called before L.A.B.A.’s executive committee on May 28 based on a complaint filed by the tournament’s sponsor, Mort Luby<\/a>. Luby is publisher of The National Bowler’s Journal <\/span>and Billiard Review<\/span>. During the hearing, Stein admitted inserting the fictitious names. The transcripts are being forwarded to the A.B.C.’s head offices in Milwaukee for a final ruling on Stein\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s status. Stein himself is en route to St. Louis, and plans to drop in on the A.B.C.\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s leaders to discuss his case.<\/p>\n

The Belleville, Illinois-bred Stein settled in Los Angeles in 1939, and has been employed as an instructor at the Sunset Center alleys. His lifetime average in 1939 was 202, and he averaged 231 when he set the all-time high score record for nine games in 1937. Reporting on his astonishing 855 series rolled at Pico Palace in October 1939 (the second highest score in forty years of A.B.C. record keeping), the Times dubbed him \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cthe sensational Jewish kegler.\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d<\/p>\n

The sensational Mr. Stein seems to have felt he was too good a bowler to be limited to a single prize package. We\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2ll have to wait and see if the bosses of bowldom agree.<\/p><\/div>\n<\/div>\n","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"
