Feb. 17, 1907
\nLos Angeles<\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

West 12th<\/sup> Street between Main and Hoover<\/a> is maddeningly crooked, but how to fix it? One set of residents has agreed to cut the boulevard through front yards because having the street as straight as an engineer\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s ruler will raise property values. The other set says that homes will be ruined and that residents will be assessed too much to pay for the work. <\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

Those in favor of the improvement include W.H. O\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2Melveny (hm. Isn\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2t that a familiar name?) while opposition is led by Mr. Kincaid, the developer of the Kincaid Tract. <\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cWhen the matter was brought up before the City Council several weeks ago, there was a merry tussle, but the side favoring the proposition won out,\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d The Times says. <\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

Alas, city planners in 1907 failed to anticipate a large sports arena blocking traffic.<\/p>\n
