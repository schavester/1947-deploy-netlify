March 9, 1907
\nLos Angeles<\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

The Insanity Begins<\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

Led by I. Newerf and J.B. Dudley, the automobile owners of Los Angeles are fighting a new city ordinance that bans parking within 40 feet of downtown <\/span>intersections. Newerf, the West Coast representative of Goodyear Tire Co., and Dudley, a car salesman, received citations for violating the law and have pleaded not guilty. <\/span><\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

In April 1909, Dudley pleaded not guilty to involuntary manslaughter after hitting street inspector Woodman J. Thomas on Broadway near 5th<\/sup> Street. He was sentenced to five years\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2 probation in March 1910. <\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

Disgraced Woman<\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

Hazel La Doux, a.k.a. Hazel Rogers, hid her face with a veil as she was tried on charges of forgery. <\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cHer downfall is said to be due to a man named William Rogers, an alleged Ascot tout who deserted her,\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d The Times says. \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cIt is charged that Miss La Doux forged the name of Mrs. John Brink on a check for $120 and cashed it.\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d <\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

La Doux told police that she and Rogers used a scheme in which she took a job with a reputable employer and passed clients\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2 information to her lover. La Doux said she worked at a department store in Oakland and a dentist\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s office in Los Angeles, turning over names to Rogers, who forged the checks. <\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cShe had been an honest woman, she said, until Rogers\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2 oily tongue and smooth ways captivated her and she became his mistress and then a thief,\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d The Times says. <\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

He Paid $40<\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

Restaurant owner Frank Flood stood over his wife, Annie, as she lay on the floor of their quarters at a Spring Street rooming house and said it would be worth the $25 fine just so he could beat her up. <\/p>\n

<\/span><\/p>\n

In testifying against him, \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cShe recited a story of shocking cruelty, saying that she had been mistreated, scorned and finally beaten by the man who promised to love, cherish and protect her,\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d The Times says. <\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

Flood did not dispute any of the charges, refused to cross-examine her (a husband\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s right in those days) and pleaded guilty to battery. \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cHe admitted that he struck her and confessed to having assaulted her with his fist as she lay on the floor,\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d The Times says. <\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

He paid $40 ($837.08 USD 2005). <\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

Flood skipped town in September 1907. \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cHe made the acquaintance of a fast sot and spent plenty of money, too much, in fact, for a man of his means. Late suppers at swell cafes cost Flood much cash. Then he became possessed of a desire to take long journeys in touring cars. He paid his bills with other people\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s money, the new restaurant manager says, by levying on the cash drawer of the restaurant, which is owned by a company,\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d The Times says. <\/span><\/p>\n
