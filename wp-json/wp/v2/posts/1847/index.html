Los Angeles
\nJan. 20, 2007<\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

What we do know about H.J. Brainerd is that he built a fair number of \u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c5\u201cportable homes.\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00c2\u009d What we don\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2t know, except in one case, is exactly where he put them.<\/p>\n

<\/a> <\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

Brainerd was active from 1906 to 1911, building homes throughout Southern California. His ads appealed to people like sportsmen, ranchers, oil executives and anyone else who might need a no-frills building put up in a few days in a relatively remote area.<\/p>\n

<\/a> <\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

In 1909, for example, Brainerd sold a three-room house to the Cerritos Gun Club, a three-room bungalow to Horace M. Dobbins for a ranch near Arcadia and a bungalow in San Diego, The Times says.<\/p>\n

<\/a><\/p>\n

\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

The only example of a Brainerd home that I\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2ve located can be found at 1158 E. 41st<\/sup> St<\/a>. Although it appears to have some sort of masonry facade, the house is of the proper vintage and resembles the few photos I have located of Brainerd homes. Of course it has a big palm tree in front, the telltale sign of a home from this era.<\/p>\n

<\/o:p><\/p>\n

If anyone has information on other Brainerd houses in the Southland (or anything about Ducker\u00c3\u00a2\u00e2\u201a\u00ac\u00e2\u201e\u00a2s Patent Homes), let me know.<\/p>\n
